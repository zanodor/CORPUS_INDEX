The principal editor Asko Parpola explains in the Preface: "Sites with more abundant seal and graffiti material were selected for the present volume, to ease the demand for space in the final volume with a large number of sites."(p.vii)
True to the selection, a large number of 'inscriptions' in the total of 1347 objects presented in Corpus Indus Script 3.2 are graffiti. Most of the graffiti illustrations do NOT contain complete depiction of 'hieroglyphs' and hence, a comparison of these selections with the rest of the Indus Script Corpora, the Sign lists and Pictorial Motifs becomes extremely difficult. However, there are some illustrations which do provide indications on the graffiti potters's marks which may

Decipherment of these hieroglyphs has been presented in the inscriptions detailed in Epigraphia Indus Script, Vols. 1 to 3. It has been demonstrated that all these hieroglphs relate to wealth accounting ledger entries related to metal work and lapidary work in daybooks of guilds and mints of Meluhha artisans and seafaring merchants.
Pottery marks on pottery graffiti of the new material (1347) in Corpus Vol. 3.2 may constitute variant orthography of the hieroglyphs of the orthographic styles listed above.

Gamesmen graffiti k449 to k455; k456 to 464; k465 to k476
The so-called gamesmen maybe terracotta lids of pots which are assigne to artisans. The hieroglyphs incised on these lids may signify contents of the jar to which the lid of the pot is related: dhakka 'lid' rebus: dhakka 'blazing, bright metal' baṭa 'rimless pot' rebus: baṭa 'iron' bhaṭa 'furnace' dāṭu 'cross' rebus: dhatu mineral ore' kanac 'corner' rebus: kancu 'bell-metal'
karã̄ n. pl. wristlets, bangles Rebus: khār 'blacksmith'
