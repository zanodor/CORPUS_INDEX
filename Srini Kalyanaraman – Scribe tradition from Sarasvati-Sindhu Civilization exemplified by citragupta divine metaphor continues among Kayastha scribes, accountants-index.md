Scribe tradition from Sarasvati-Sindhu Civilization exemplified by citragupta divine metaphor continues among Kayastha scribes, accountants
There are two extraordinary essays in two encyclopaedic texts of Bharat: -वाचस्पत्यम् , शब्द कल्प द्र ुमः| These essays detailing the tradition of Kayastha, 'scribes' are appended.
The tradition is emphatic that a scribe is a person versed in अक्षरा akṣarā Sound, word,
speech. He or she is also an artisan, artificer. A synonym for the scribe, अक्षरचणः is kayastha or citragupta. Kayastha is an accountant. Citragupta lit.means 'secret writing in pictures'. That the Indus Script is alluded to in ancient texts of India comes from three subjects taught to the youth, according to Vātsyāyana's Vidyāsamuddes'a. The three arts taught to the youth are:
1. des'a bhāshā jnānam 'knowledge of dialects,or variants in speech forms of words' 2. अक्षरमुष्टिका kathanam 'communicating syllables or ideas by the fingers' 3. mlecchita vikalpa
That the accountants invented the writing system of Indus Script is surmised from the fact that almost all the 8000+ inscriptions of the Indus Script Corpora are wealth-accounting ledgers.
There is a pun in the expression अ-क्षर --मुष्टिका kathanam. The word मुष्टिका means 'wrist'; it also signifies by a 'goldsmith'. It is seen that most of the wealth-creating activities documented on Indus Script inscriptions relate to the work of goldsmiths. The most frequently used pictorial motifs on inscriptions is that of a forward-thrusting, spiny-horned young bull fronting a 'standard device composed of two parts -- 1. lathe; 2. portable gold furnace'.The hieroglyphs signify singhin 'forward-thrusting, spiny-horned' rebus: singi 'ornament gold' PLUS kunda 'lathe' rebus: kunda 'fine gold' PLUS kammata 'portable gold furnace' rebus: kammata 'mint, coiner, coinage'. Thus,most of the inscriptions signify an artisan working with fine gold (24 carat) and ornament gold (22 carat by alloying fine gold with silver or copper). Thus, the expression अ-क्षर -
मुष्टिका kathanam can also be interpreted to mean: story of goldsmiths
documenting अक्षरा akṣarā Sound, word, speech of their wealth-creation activities.
Mlecchita vikalpa lit. means 'an alternative (written representation) by mleccha artisans'. Mleccha has two meanings: 1. mleccha,milakkhu 'copper'; 2. mleccha, meluhha, 'a person who mispronounces words or used ungrammatical speech'. Thus,mlecchita signifies spoken forms of words. This is called in linguistics, 'sprachbund' or speech union which is a way to identify cognate words among varieties of dialects but with the same underlying meanings of words. Linguists are now agreed that Ancient India was a sprachbund, 'speech union' where speakers from various language families lived and interacted with one another and absorbed features of languages from one another and made them their own. This is referred to as 'linguistic area'. The decipherment of Indus Script has demonstrated that the words used to document activities of artisans, seafaring merchants, lapidary-metalwork guilds are signified by hieroglyphs, a form of citragupta, 'picture writing cipher'.

This Indus Script Cipher lives on and continues to be used on millions of ancient coins from hundreds of mints of Sarasvati-Sindhu Civilization. Thus, the decipherment has conclusively demonstrated that the writing sytem of hieroglyphs was based on the underlying spoken forms of languages of the artisans and seafaring merchants of guilds of Sarasvati-Sindhu Civilization.
A splendid example of the continuing tradition of Indus Script Cipher is seen on Bharhut and Sanchi Stupa torana-s or gateways which are adorned with Indus Script hieroglyphs.
Bharhut torana Sanchi torana
The hieroglyph composition common to both torana is a pair of fish-fin joined together with the fith-tails shown curved upwards. The Meluhha rebus reading is: aya 'fish' rebus: aya 'iron' ayas 'alloy metal' PLUS khambhaṛā 'fish-fin' rebus: Ta. kampaṭṭam coinage, coin. Ma. kammaṭṭam, kammiṭṭam coinage, mint. Ka. kammaṭa id.; kammaṭi a coiner. (DEDR 1236). Thus, the rebus reading of the hypertext is ayo kammata, 'alloy metals mint'.This expression is attested in Mahavamsa XXV,28 uses the expression: ayo kammata.dvara, 'gateway of alloy metals mint', to signify the toranas of Sanchi and Bharhut. The guild-master shown on Sanchi torana is śrēṣṭhin khār. This title is signified by the Indus Script hieroglyph: 'palm squirrel' orthographically stylized as a 'sign' of the script.
