This relief of a lion battling a bull is seen at the ancient throne room of Darius in his palace at Persepolis, the seat of his vast Achaemenid Empire. The lion is seen in Persepolis as the symbol of absolute power and heroic triumph. The bull is also a symbol of power in ancient Persia and is seen throughout Persepolis on top of columns and guarding gates. The magnificent palace complex at Persepolis was founded by Darius the Great around 518 BCE. https://www.bible-history.com/archaeology/persia/persepolis-lion.html



Lydian electrum coin (one-third stater), one of the oldest known coins, early 6th century BCE https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrum#/media/File:BMC_06.jpg The hieroglyph on the forehead of the lion signifies: arka 'sun's rays' rebus: arka 'copper, gold'.
The display's earliest piece is an electrum coin, 6th century BCE. Fitzwilliam Museum, UK From the kingdom of Lydia (western Turkey), it depicts a lion and bull in combat. An early reference to coinage records a payment in coin by the Lydian king for a military purpose. http://bit.ly/2Aaf5JB
The feline paw on the coin is an Indus script hieroglyph: panja 'feline paw' rebus: panja 'kiln, furnace' Note: Electrum coin of Lydia is a clear depiction of the arrival of the gold standard, making a distinction between fine or pure gold and ornament gold, i.e. 24 carat and 22 carat gold respectively.
