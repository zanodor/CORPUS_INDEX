One example of unambiguous orthography in Indus Script corpora is provided by a one-eyed person holding back rearing tigers with outstretched hands.
The addendum revises the earlier readings by identifying the Meluhha hieroglyph expressions and rebus Meluhha readings for a 'one-eyed person' and 'six hair-knots'.
.काणा kāṇā 'one-eye' (Marathi) Rebus: काणा kāṇā 'wealth, riches, possession, goods'. The word explains the exclusive land-ownership by the related expression, kāṇi 'Landed property, estate, possession'. Six hair-knots are signified as a hieroglyph by bata 'six' PLUS konta 'hair-knot'; ग ोंध gōndha f Combings of the hair. 2 fig. A knotty or complicate case; a
perplexed affair (Marathi) Rebus: क ोंड kōṇḍa m C A circular hedge or field-fence;A
circular hamlet; a division of a मौजा or village, composed generally of the huts of one caste. 5
Grounds under one occupancy or tenancy. (Or क ोंडी q. v.) A confined place gen.; a lock-up house &c. (Marathi)
Thus the rebus Meluhha renderings of the set of hieroglyphs signify land under one occupancy or tenancy, wealth of possession and goods.
Holding back the rearing tigers is signified by the word क ोंडणें kōṇḍaṇēm, क ोंदणें kōndaṇēm
'To be stuffed, obstructed, blocked up' Rebus: determinative of the word क ोंड kōṇḍa 'a circular hamlet or a hamlet of artisans (guild)'. kola 'tiger' rebus: kol 'working in iron' kolhe 'smelter'. Thus, together, the orthography of the hypertext composed of hieroglyphs signifies wealth, goods, possessions from iron smelter, furnace work -- in summary, a wealth accounting ledger entry created by the scribe.
On one tablet the one-eyed person is further elaborated by the hieroglyphs of spoked wheel and elephant; the rebus readings are: kāṇá 'one-eye' rebus: kāṇá 'wealth, goods, possessions' PLUS tsarkh 'potter's wheel' rebus: arka 'copper, gold' ibha 'elephant' rebus: ib 'iron' karibha 'elephant' rebus: karba 'iron'.

This hieroglyph of backbone is an allograph of the 'one-eye' hieroglyph.
